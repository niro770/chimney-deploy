Why Baltimore Residents Choose Us

Baltimore's row house culture — the city has more row homes per capita than any other American city — creates a unique chimney landscape. Formstone-faced and brick row houses in Hampden, Federal Hill, Canton and Fells Point were built primarily between 1880–1940, giving Baltimore one of the oldest active chimney systems of any major East Coast city. The Chesapeake Bay's humidity combined with Baltimore's freeze-thaw winter cycle is particularly damaging: moisture penetrates brick and mortar, freezes, expands and cracks — a process that repeats dozens of times per winter season. Baltimore's proximity to the Bay also means chimney caps corrode faster from salt air than in inland markets.

Baltimore's chimney problems are amplified by decades of deferred maintenance in the city's older housing stock. Many row homes purchased for renovation in Hampden, Remington and Reservoir Hill contain original unlined chimney flues — some of which were last professionally inspected decades ago, if ever. Gas inserts added by previous owners frequently vent through flues that were never relined to appropriate specifications. This is particularly common in Baltimore's gentrifying neighborhoods where older homes have changed hands multiple times without proper chimney disclosure.

When to book: Baltimore's chimney season runs October through March. The Chesapeake Bay's moderating influence means Baltimore winters are slightly milder than Philadelphia, but freeze-thaw cycling is still significant. Book in September for pre-season availability.
